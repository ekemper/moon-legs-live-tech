# 07 — Hand Independence

**Hand independence** means each hand can do something different at the same time—different rhythms, dynamics, or articulations. It’s one of the biggest challenges for beginners and is essential for most piano music (melody in one hand, accompaniment in the other).

---

## Why it’s hard

- **Natural tendency:** We want both hands to copy each other (same rhythm, same volume).
- **Coordination:** The brain has to send different instructions to each hand at the same time.
- **Building block:** Many pieces use melody (right hand) and accompaniment (left hand), so you need to train this early.

---

## When to start

Start hand-independence work **after** you can:

- Play a C major scale (one octave) with each hand.
- Play legato and staccato comfortably in each hand.

If those are still shaky, keep practicing [04 Five-finger position](04-five-finger-position-and-pentascales.md), [05 Scales](05-scales-and-thumb-technique.md), and [06 Articulation](06-articulation-legato-and-staccato.md) first.

---

## Exercise 1: Different dynamics (loud vs quiet)

**Goal:** One hand loud, the other quiet; then switch.

**How:**

1. Play the **C major pentascale** (or one-octave C major scale) with **both hands together** in unison (same notes, same time).
2. **Right hand loud, left hand quiet:** Play the scale again; right hand *forte* (loud), left hand *piano* (soft). Keep a steady pulse.
3. **Left hand loud, right hand quiet:** Reverse. Left hand *forte*, right hand *piano*.
4. Repeat several times. Focus on keeping the rhythm steady while the hands do different volumes.

**Why it helps:** You learn to control each hand’s volume separately—like melody (louder) and accompaniment (softer) in real pieces.

---

## Exercise 2: Different articulation (legato vs staccato)

**Goal:** One hand legato, the other staccato; then switch.

**How:**

1. Play the **C major pentascale** with both hands in unison.
2. **Right hand legato, left hand staccato:** Play the scale again. Right hand smooth and connected; left hand short and detached. Go up and down.
3. **Left hand legato, right hand staccato:** Switch. Left hand legato; right hand staccato.
4. Repeat. Keep a steady tempo; don’t let one hand drag the other.

**Why it helps:** You get used to different “jobs” in each hand—smooth melody in one, crisp accompaniment in the other.

---

## Exercise 3: Canon (one hand follows the other)

**Goal:** One hand leads; the other plays the same thing one or two beats (or one bar) later.

**How:**

1. Choose a **short melody** you know well in one hand (e.g. first phrase of “Frère Jacques” or “Mary Had a Little Lamb,” or a simple 4-note pattern like C–D–E–C).
2. **Right hand leads:** Play the melody with the right hand. As soon as the right hand starts, the **left hand** plays the **same melody** one or two beats (or one bar) later. Both hands play the same notes, but staggered.
3. Keep a steady pulse. When the right hand reaches the end, the left hand is still catching up; let the left hand finish.
4. **Left hand leads:** Same idea, but left hand leads and right hand follows.

**Why it helps:** This is a step toward Bach-style two-part writing and helps you hear and feel two lines at once.

---

## Practice tips

- **Slow:** Do every exercise **slowly** at first. Speed comes later.
- **Hands separate first:** If an exercise is too hard, practice each hand’s part alone until it’s automatic, then combine.
- **Metronome:** Use a metronome to keep a steady beat so one hand doesn’t slow down or speed up the other.
- **Short sessions:** A few minutes of hand-independence work per day is enough. Consistency matters more than length.

---

## What to expect

- At first it will feel awkward. That’s normal.
- Start with **dynamics** (loud/quiet), then **articulation** (legato/staccato), then **canon**.
- Over weeks, your brain will get used to sending different instructions to each hand. Keep at it.

---

## Quick reference

- **Dynamics:** One hand loud, one quiet; then switch. (Melody vs accompaniment.)
- **Articulation:** One hand legato, one staccato; then switch.
- **Canon:** One hand leads; the other plays the same melody one bar (or two beats) later.
- **Practice:** Slow, steady pulse; hands separate first if needed; short daily sessions.
